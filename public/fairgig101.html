01. The 2:00 AM Envelope

The End of the
Blind Gig

It’s 2:00 AM. You’re hauling a 60-pound Twin Reverb down a narrow flight of stairs. Your ears are ringing, your fingers are raw, and your shirt smells like stale beer and floor cleaner. As you load the last cymbal stand into a cramped van, the promoter hands you a crumpled white envelope.


You open it under the streetlamp. Forty bucks.


Did you even make enough to cover the gas? What happened to the door money? Where did the ticket presales go? The promoter mumbles something about "sound guy fees" and "marketing overhead" before disappearing back inside. For the independent artist, this isn't a business. It’s an abusive relationship. Deals are struck on vague text threads, and the math remains a black box until the night is over. You aren't treated like talent; you are treated like an unsecured, zero-interest lender to struggling venues.

02. The Big Lie

Discovery
is a Trap

The music tech world loves to sell us on "discovery." They tell you that if you just get on the right app and find the right stage, your career will explode. This is the industry’s Big Lie.


Finding a stage isn't the problem. Getting paid without getting played is the problem.


The industry has quietly shifted all the financial risk onto the backs of the artists. "Pay-to-play," hidden production fees, and opaque door splits are bleeding the working musician dry. Venues don't need another generic booking app. Artists need a weapon to force the truth into the light.

03. Genesis

From Sanctuary to
War Room

FairGig wasn’t built in a Silicon Valley boardroom. It was built by someone who lived the grind. Our founder, Cliff Franco, has been inseparable from the guitar since a visit to Jimi Hendrix’s grave in 1999.


Over 15 years fronting bands, music transitioned from a hobby to his personal sanctuary—a sacred space to keep his mind aligned. But every time he stepped off the stage and tried to settle up with a venue, the predatory reality of the business violated that sanctuary.


He didn't build software to start a tech company. He built FairGig to build a wall around the music, keeping the parasites out.

04. The Math

Weaponize
Your Draw

To fix a rigged game, you have to change the rules. FairGig replaces industry guesswork with cold, hard math. We built the Show Engine, but you can think of it as an X-Ray machine for your gigs.


Instead of nodding along to a promoter's verbal promises, you use simple sliders on your phone to input the deal. Instantly, the app calculates the break-even point. It shows you exactly what the band takes home versus what the venue pockets if the room is empty, half-full, or sold out.


Suddenly, you aren't just a band hoping for a gig. You are a business presenting mathematical proof of the revenue you generate. You can finally weaponize your draw. "Take it or leave it" offers evaporate when you hold the math in your hand.

05. The Truth Tax

Exposing the
Ghosts

FairGig is completely free for artists, and that is by design. We are building an army of independent bands, touring acts, and local legends who simply refuse to play in the dark anymore. When an entire community of musicians demands transparency, venues have nowhere to hide.


FairGig tracks venue behavior through a Reputation Layer:

Fairness Score: Do they hide fees?
Payout Reliability: Do they actually pay what they promised?
Artist Ratings: Real talk from the bands who stood on their stage.

When transparency becomes the standard, a venue’s refusal to use FairGig becomes a massive red flag. Good venues and honest promoters—the ones who gladly show bands exactly where the money goes—will attract the best talent. The shady promoters will find themselves playing to empty rooms.

06. Conclusion

The Bottom Line

The era of the "blind gig" is over. We are dragging the live music economy out of the shadows.


We are done acting like gamblers whose stakes are our own livelihoods. The question for venues is no longer whether they want to be transparent, but whether they can afford to be left behind by the artists who draw the crowds.


Before FairGig, you guessed. Now, you know. Let’s take our stages back.
