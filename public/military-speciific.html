Strategic Recommendations

Mitigating Risk &
Maintaining US Maritime Presence

1. Executive Summary

As the strategic environment becomes increasingly contested, the United States Navy and Coast Guard must balance the imperative of maintaining a robust global maritime presence with the need to mitigate the risk of unintended escalation. This document outlines a comprehensive stabilization strategy focused on adjusting Rules of Engagement (ROE), strengthening bilateral and multilateral communication channels, and rapidly integrating technology-driven surveillance alternatives to reduce the exposure of manned platforms.

02
ROE_ADJ // TACTICAL

2. Rules of Engagement (ROE) Adjustments

To minimize tactical miscalculations while preserving the right to self-defense and freedom of navigation, ROE should be adapted to emphasize graduated, non-kinetic responses and strict escalation control.

Graduated Non-Kinetic Warning Protocols: Standardize the use of directed acoustic devices (e.g., LRAD), laser warning receivers, and dazzlers as primary tools for signaling boundaries and deterring aggressive maneuvers by adversarial vessels (e.g., maritime militia or fast attack craft) before transitioning to kinetic warning shots.
Dynamic Standoff Zones: Implement dynamically adjustable proximity triggers based on the assessed threat environment. Establish clear, pre-communicated minimum standoff distances for manned vessels, utilizing autonomous screening vessels as an outer perimeter to absorb initial probing actions.
Pre-Delegation for Defensive Electronic Warfare (EW): Adjust ROE to pre-delegate authority for non-destructive electronic attack (EA) and jamming measures against adversarial drones and surveillance assets, allowing tactical commanders to neutralize immediate surveillance threats without crossing the threshold of lethal force.
Behavior-Based Triggers over Platform-Based Triggers: Shift tactical ROE guidance to evaluate the behavior of an approaching vessel rather than strictly its classification (e.g., treating gray-zone maritime militia vessels exhibiting hostile intent with the same defensive protocols as conventional naval combatants).
03
COMMS // DE-ESCALATION

3. Communication Channels and Crisis De-escalation

Effective tactical and strategic communication is paramount to preventing "gray zone" incidents from spiraling into conventional conflict.

Mandatory Adherence to CUES: Enforce strict adherence to the Code for Unplanned Encounters at Sea (CUES) across all manned surface and airborne assets. Proactively broadcast intentions in clear, standardized language via VHF bridge-to-bridge communications to reduce ambiguity.
Tactical-Level Hotlines: Establish and operationalize direct, encrypted tactical data and voice links with rival maritime operational commands in high-risk zones (e.g., South China Sea, Persian Gulf) to allow immediate de-escalation by local commanders rather than relying solely on slow strategic-level channels.
Mil-to-Mil Crisis Communication: Advocate for the maintenance and routine testing of high-level military-to-military crisis communication channels. Ensure these lines bypass political bottlenecks to allow operational commanders to quickly clarify the intent behind sudden force posture changes.
Public Signaling and Transparency: Utilize real-time commercial satellite imagery and unclassified sensor feeds to rapidly declassify and broadcast unsafe or unprofessional behavior by adversarial actors. This "naming and shaming" strategy deters aggressive actions while maintaining the high ground in the information domain.

TECH // ISR_AUTONOMY

4. Technology-Driven Surveillance Alternatives

Replacing or augmenting manned patrols with unmanned systems reduces the risk to US personnel, lowers the operational footprint, and provides persistent ISR (Intelligence, Surveillance, and Reconnaissance) capabilities.

Proliferation of Unmanned Surface Vessels (USVs)

Scale initiatives similar to NAVCENT's Task Force 59. Deploy fleets of long-endurance USVs (e.g., Saildrones, Sea Hunters) equipped with radar, AIS, and optical sensors. These assets can maintain a persistent forward presence, absorb aggressive blocking maneuvers, and provide early warning without risking human lives.

Unmanned Underwater Vehicles (UUVs) for Covert Presence

Expand the use of large and extra-large UUVs (XLUUVs) for anti-submarine warfare (ASW) surveillance and chokepoint monitoring. UUVs allow the US to maintain a stealthy, persistent presence in highly contested anti-access/area-denial (A2/AD) environments where manned vessels would be highly vulnerable.

High-Altitude Long Endurance (HALE) Aerial Surveillance

Leverage platforms like the MQ-4C Triton and MQ-9B SeaGuardian for broad-area maritime surveillance. These platforms provide over-the-horizon targeting and vessel tracking, reducing the need for forward-deployed Arleigh Burke-class destroyers simply for ISR purposes.

Commercial Satellite Integration and AI

Deepen integration with commercial space-based ISR providers (e.g., Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) and RF mapping constellations like HawkEye 360). Utilize Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning (AI/ML) to fuse this data with AIS tracks, automatically identifying "dark targets" (vessels turning off AIS) and anomalies in maritime traffic, queuing unmanned assets to investigate rather than dispatching a manned ship.

5. Conclusion

By adopting graduated ROE focused on non-kinetic deterrence, enforcing robust tactical communication protocols, and aggressively transitioning routine ISR missions to an unmanned and space-based architecture, the US military can effectively maintain its global maritime presence.

This hybrid approach ensures freedom of navigation and deters malign actors while significantly minimizing the risk of strategic miscalculation and the exposure of manned forces.
