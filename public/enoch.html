Extensive Research Monograph

The Book of Enoch

( 1 ENOCH )

The Book of Enoch (specifically 1 Enoch; Hebrew: סֵפֶר חֲנוֹךְ, Sēfer Ḥănōḵ; Ge'ez: መጽሐፈ ሄኖክ, Maṣḥafa Hēnok) is one of the most significant, complex, and influential non-canonical apocalyptic texts to survive from the Second Temple period. Rather than a singular composition, it is an ancient Jewish "library" containing five distinct booklets written by different authors at different times (spanning from roughly 300 BCE to 100 BCE).

For centuries, the complete text was lost to the Western and Eastern Roman worlds, surviving only in Ge'ez (Classical Ethiopic) within the canon of the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church. However, the mid-20th-century discovery of the Dead Sea Scrolls at Qumran revolutionized Enochic scholarship, confirming its Aramaic origin, its deep popularity among ancient Jews, and its profound influence on the theological matrix of early Christianity.

1. Structural Breakdown of 1 Enoch (The Five Booklets)

Scholars agree that 1 Enoch is a composite work. Its structure reflects a literary evolution, with various sections serving as separate theological pamphlets before being compiled into a single "Pentateuch" of Enochic literature.

I. The Book of the Watchers

Chapters 1–36 | Est. Date: c. 3rd Century BCE

Summary: This section acts as a massive midrash (expansion) on the highly enigmatic passage in Genesis 6:1–4, which describes the "sons of God" mating with the "daughters of men."

  • The Fall of the Angels: Led by Semjaza and Azazel, a group of two hundred high-ranking angels known as the Watchers (Egrēgori) swear an oath on Mount Hermon to descend to Earth, take human wives, and father a race of violent, monstrous giants called the Nephilim.
  • The Origin of Evil (Forbidden Knowledge): The Watchers corrupt humanity by introducing forbidden celestial arts. Azazel teaches metalworking, weaponry, and cosmetics (prompting vanity and warfare); Semjaza teaches sorcery and root-cutting; others teach astrology, astronomy, and writing. This places the origin of human sin on cosmic, angelic rebellion rather than the transgression of Adam and Eve.
  • Enoch’s Intercession: The dying Earth cries out to heaven. God sentences the Watchers to be bound in darkness under the earth and their giant offspring to destroy one another. The Watchers beg Enoch to write a petition to God for their forgiveness. God rejects the petition, and Enoch is taken on a series of journeys through the cosmos, guided by archangels (Uriel, Raphael, Michael, Gabriel), where he sees Sheol, the fiery prison of the fallen angels, and the tree of life.

II. The Book of Parables / Similitudes

Chapters 37–71 | Est. Date: c. late 1st C. BCE to early 1st C. CE

Special Note: This is the only section of 1 Enoch that was not found among the Dead Sea Scrolls at Qumran, suggesting a later date or a different provenance (possibly Galilee).

  • The "Son of Man" / The Chosen One: This section contains highly developed Messianic imagery. Enoch beholds a pre-existent, divine figure called the "Son of Man," "the Chosen One," or "the Righteous One." He sits on a throne of glory alongside the "Lord of Spirits" to judge the quick and the dead. This section provides the direct linguistic and theological framework for Jesus’ self-designation in the Gospels.
  • Eschatological Reversal: It heavily condemns "the kings and the mighty"—the earthly rulers and wealthy elite who oppress the righteous poor. They are stripped of their power and cast into judgment.
  • Resurrection: This section features one of the earliest explicit references in Jewish literature to a bodily resurrection of both the righteous and the wicked.

III. The Astronomical Book / Book of Luminaries

Chapters 72–82 | Est. Date: c. 3rd Century BCE

Summary: Guided by the archangel Uriel, Enoch is shown the mechanics of the heavens, detail-mapping the sun, moon, stars, and winds as they pass through specific "gates" in the eastern and western horizons.

  • The 364-Day Solar Calendar: The primary goal of this booklet is polemical. It argues fiercely for a strict 364-day solar calendar (divisible by 7, meaning holy days fall on the exact same day of the week every year) over the 354-day lunar calendar used by the mainstream temple priesthood in Jerusalem. The author argues that deviating from the 364-day cycle causes humanity to miss holy festivals, corrupting the ritual order.
  • Cosmic Sympathy: The book warns that when humanity descends into sin in the end times, the natural order will revolt—rains will be withheld, crops will fail, and celestial bodies will alter their paths out of cosmic misalignment.

IV. The Book of Dream Visions

Chapters 83–90 | Est. Date: c. 167–160 BCE

Summary: Enoch recounts two highly symbolic dreams to his son, Methuselah. The first is a vision of the coming Great Flood. The second is the famous Animal Apocalypse.

  • The Animal Apocalypse: This is a coded, allegorical retelling of human history from Adam to the final judgment. Characters are represented zoomorphically:
    • Bulls: The patriarchs (Adam is a white bull; Noah is a white bull that becomes a human; Abraham is a white bull).
    • Sheep: The Israelites/the righteous remnant.
    • Wild Beasts & Birds: Israel's Gentile enemies (Egyptians are wolves; Philistines are dogs; Babylonians are lions; Seleucid Greeks are eagles and ravens).
    • Angels: White men.
  • The Seventy Shepherds: The text explains Israel’s suffering during the exile by alleging that God handed the "sheep" over to seventy angelic guardians ("shepherds") who acted lawlessly, allowing Israel's enemies to slaughter more sheep than God had authorized.
  • The Horned Sheep: The vision culminates with the rise of a "sheep with a great horn" (representing Judas Maccabeus), who rallies the flock to fight the eagles. A great sword is given to the sheep, and a "white bull with large horns" (the Messiah) is born, to whom all wild beasts submit.

V. The Epistle of Enoch

Chapters 91–108 | Est. Date: c. 2nd Century BCE

Summary: Formulated as a farewell discourse or ethical testament from Enoch to his children and future generations. It includes warnings, pronouncements of "Woes" on the wicked, and a historic-prophetic outline.

  • The Apocalypse of Weeks (Chs. 91, 93): A highly structured summary of human history and destiny, dividing time into ten "weeks" (epochs). Weeks 1–7 cover past history; Weeks 8–10 detail the future (rise of a righteous remnant, destruction of the world, new heaven).
  • Ethical Dualism: Heavily contrasts the righteous poor with the wicked rich, predicting immediate and terrifying cosmic justice for those who rely on ill-gotten wealth.
  • The Birth of Noah (Chs. 106–107): Often classed as a "Noahic Fragment". At birth, Noah’s body is "white as snow and red as a rose," and his hair is like wool; when he opens his eyes, he lights up the entire house like the sun. His father, Lamech, is terrified that Noah is actually the son of a Watcher. Enoch reassures Lamech that the child is fully human and destined to save a remnant from the coming global judgment.

2. The Dead Sea Scrolls
& the Aramaic Original

For centuries, Western scholars debated whether 1 Enoch was originally written in Greek, Hebrew, or Aramaic. The discovery of the Dead Sea Scrolls at Qumran Cave 4 between 1947 and 1956 settled this definitively.

The Qumran Manuscripts

Scribes at Qumran preserved eleven Aramaic manuscripts of various parts of 1 Enoch (labeled 4Q201–202, 4Q204–212), alongside several Hebrew/Aramaic fragments of the Astronomical Book (4Q208–211). The density of copies shows that for the Essene sect, the Enochic library was treated with a status equivalent to, or perhaps exceeding, books of the Hebrew Bible.

The Mystery of the Missing "Book of Parables"

Intriguingly, not a single fragment of the Book of Parables (Chs. 37–71) was found at Qumran. Two main scholarly schools of thought:

  • The Late Composition Theory: Scribes did not copy it because it did not exist yet; composed late 1st century CE.
  • The Sectarian Exclusion Theory: The "Son of Man" messianism conflicted with the Qumran sect’s expectation of two distinct Messiahs (priestly and royal).

What is the Book of Giants?

Pioneering Qumran scholar Józef Milik hypothesized that the original Aramaic Enochic collection was structured as a five-book "Pentateuch," but with the Book of Giants instead of the Book of Parables. When translated by later Christians, the highly mythological Book of Giants was replaced by the Christological Parables. The Aramaic fragments reveal a bizarre narrative:

  • Mesopotamian Syncretism: The text names several giants, including Ohya, Hahya, and Mahway. Astonishingly, one of the giants is named Gilgamesh—the legendary hero of the ancient Mesopotamian epic. This shows a direct fusion of Babylonian mythology and Jewish apocalyptic thought.
  • Apocalyptic Nightmares: The giants suffer terrifying, prophetic dreams of a garden submerged in water, leaving only three shoots (the Deluge and Noah).
  • Flight to Enoch: Desperate, the giants send Mahway, who flies across the heavens to locate Enoch in his remote sanctuary. Enoch translates the dreams as a final, unalterable sentence of physical destruction, though their disembodied spirits will remain on Earth to torment humanity (origin of demons).

3. Influence on the New Testament

The New Testament does not exist in a vacuum; it was written by authors immersed in Second Temple apocalyptic thought. The Book of Enoch left an indelible mark on early Christian theology.

The Epistle of Jude

Jude does not simply reference Enoch; he utilizes a classic Jewish formula of scriptural citation: "Enoch... prophesied, saying..." confirming he viewed 1 Enoch as divinely inspired.

"It was also about these that Enoch, the seventh from Adam, prophesied, saying: 'See, the Lord is coming with ten thousands of his holy ones...'"
— Jude 1:14-15
"And behold! He cometh with ten thousands of holy ones..."
— 1 Enoch 1:9

The Bound Angels (Jude 6 & 2 Pet 2:4)

Both epistles reference angels kept in eternal chains in deep darkness. This draws directly from the sentence passed on Semjaza and Azazel in 1 Enoch 10:4–12, where Raphael casts Azazel into the darkness of Dudael.

The "Son of Man" and the Gospels

In the Gospels, Jesus uses "Son of Man" as a pre-existent, divine, and judging cosmic figure (e.g., Mark 14:62). This specific synthesis of Daniel's symbol with a throne-sharing divine judge is the primary theology developed in the Book of Parables (1 Enoch 46–48).

4. Patristic Reception & the "Sethite" Shift

During the first three centuries, 1 Enoch's status was highly debated. Early Fathers used it for angelology, but later theology rejected its literalism. 

Patristic Era (2nd–3rd c.)                          Post-Nicene Era (4th–5th c.)
- Tertullian: "Scriptura Enoch"                     - Augustine: Rejects "Watchers" literalism
- Justin Martyr: Basis for Demonology               - Developed "Sethite" Theory
- Clement: Accepted Jude's citation                 - Jerome: Explicitly labels Enoch "Apocrypha"

I. The Peak of Acceptance

  • Justin Martyr: Used the Watchers to construct doctrine of demons; pagan gods are disembodied Nephilim spirits.
  • Tertullian: Most ardent advocate. Called it "Scriptura Enoch" and defended its survival via Noah's Ark and its Apostolicity via Jude.

II. The Fourth-Century Shift

By the 4th/5th centuries, theologians like Augustine popularized the "Sethite" interpretation of Genesis 6: The "sons of God" were righteous descendants of Seth, and "daughters of men" were wicked descendants of Cain. Angels physically mating with women became heretical.

"Let us omit the fables of those writings which are called apocryphal... they have no canonical authority on account of the many false things they contain."
— Augustine, The City of God

Jerome formally classified 1 Enoch as apocryphal. Enoch was discarded, cast out of copying rotations, and effectively lost to Western history for over a millennium.

5. Canonical Survival: The Ethiopic Church

While the Roman, Byzantine, and Syriac churches suppressed 1 Enoch, one ancient Christian empire preserved it in its entirety: the Kingdom of Aksum.

Ethiopian Orthodox Church

Translated from Greek to Ge'ez during the golden age of monastic translation (4th-5th c.). Developed in geographic isolation from Mediterranean councils (like Laodicea) which forbade non-canonical books. Unaffected by the "Sethite" shift, they placed it in their "narrower canon" of 81 books.

Beta Israel & Rediscovery

The Beta Israel (Ethiopian Jews) also preserved it in their holy scriptures (Orit), proving its authority predated Christian-Jewish divergence in the region. In 1773, Scottish explorer James Bruce acquired three complete Ge'ez manuscripts, bringing them to Europe and ending 1,500 years of Western obscurity.

8. Cosmology & Angelology of 1 Enoch

Perhaps no aspect of 1 Enoch has proven more theologically fertile than its elaborately detailed cosmic geography and its systematic ordering of the angelic and demonic realms — frameworks that became foundational to both Jewish mysticism and Christian theology.

A. The Cosmic Geography: Mountains, Gates & the Ends of the Earth

In the Book of the Watchers (Chs. 17–36), Enoch is transported on a series of guided tours across the entirety of the cosmos. Unlike any biblical vision, these chapters constitute an actual cosmological map — the universe as a bounded, architecturally structured space governed by divine law.

  • The Seven Mountains of Fire (Ch. 18): Enoch beholds seven magnificent mountains of precious stone in the northwest. The central mountain reaches to heaven and its summit is a throne of God. This locale connects to the later description of the Tree of Life, whose fruit will be given to the righteous at the final judgment, filling them with an indescribable fragrance.
  • The Prison of the Stars (Ch. 18): Beyond these mountains, Enoch sees a void of chaos — a place without sky or earth — where great blazing stars are bound in chains. The angel Uriel reveals that these are the stars that transgressed God's command and failed to rise at their appointed time. This prison for disobedient stars prefigures the prison of the Watchers, suggesting a cosmic principle: all creation operates under binding covenantal law.
  • Sheol & the Three Hollow Places (Ch. 22): Enoch is shown a high mountain in the west containing a vast hollow place with three separate compartments for the dead, awaiting judgment. The souls of the righteous dwell in a spring of water with light; the souls of sinners (who were not punished on Earth) dwell in pain; the souls of sinners (who were punished in life) wait in a third, dry hollow. This represents one of the earliest systematic topographies of the afterlife in Jewish literature.
  • The Paradise of Righteousness (Ch. 32): Enoch reaches the ends of the Earth and is shown the Garden of Eden in the Far East, still existing as a living place guarded by the archangel Raphael, containing the Tree of Wisdom (the tree whose fruit Eve and Adam ate) and the Tree of Life.

B. The Archangel Hierarchy: The Holy Watchers

1 Enoch is the primary ancient text that developed the concept of a defined hierarchy of named archangels — individual divine beings with specific cosmic portfolios. This framework was entirely absent from the Hebrew Bible (where angels are largely anonymous) but is assumed throughout the New Testament.

Archangel I

Michael

Portfolio: Mercy, patience, and the nation of Israel. Oversees the best part of humanity and chaos. In 1 Enoch 10:11–14, he is commanded by God to bind Semjaza and the Watchers in the valleys of the Earth for 70 generations until the final judgment. He is the protector of the righteous dead.

Archangel II

Raphael

Portfolio: Healing and the spirits of men. Commanded to bind Azazel hand and foot and cast him into the darkness of Dudael, covering him with rocks, until the Day of Judgment (1 Enoch 10:4–8). Also serves as Enoch's primary guide through Sheol and the compartments of the dead. His name means "God heals."

Archangel III

Gabriel

Portfolio: Paradise, the Serpents, and the Cherubim. Sent against the Nephilim giants directly, commanding them to turn on each other in internecine warfare (1 Enoch 10:9). Gabriel functions as God's instrument of violent judgment where Raphael and Michael operate through binding and restraint. His name means "God is my strength."

Archangel IV

Uriel

Portfolio: The world and Tartarus. Serves as Enoch's primary guide through the Astronomical Book (Chs. 72–82), explaining the mechanics of the sun, moon, and stars. He oversees the prison of the disobedient stars and reveals the divine calendar. Uriel is almost entirely absent from the Hebrew Bible — 1 Enoch is the source of his identity. His name means "God is my light."

C. The Origin of Demons: A Systematic Demonology

One of 1 Enoch's most consequential and theologically innovative contributions is its systematic explanation for the origin of evil spirits — a question the Hebrew Bible leaves almost entirely unaddressed.

The Enochic Answer

"And now the giants who were born from the union of spirits and flesh will be called evil spirits upon the earth, and on the earth will be their dwelling place. Evil spirits proceed from their bodies... They will rise up against the children of men and against the women, for they have proceeded from them."
— 1 Enoch 15:8–9

The logic is precise and revolutionary: the Nephilim giants were hybrid beings — part angelic spirit, part human flesh. When God destroyed their physical bodies in the Flood, they could not return to heaven (their angelic nature was corrupted), nor could they rest in Sheol (their physical nature was destroyed). They therefore became disembodied spirits condemned to roam the Earth, seeking to afflict, possess, and destroy humanity until the final judgment. This is the origin of the concept of demons as it appears throughout the New Testament.

Azazel: Scapegoat Theology

The Watchers leader Azazel appears to be a direct mythological expansion of the mysterious Azazel of Leviticus 16 — the entity to whom the ancient Israelite scapegoat was dispatched into the wilderness on Yom Kippur. In 1 Enoch, Azazel is explicitly held solely responsible for all the sins of the world: "to him ascribe all sin" (1 Enoch 10:8). This represents a striking cosmic theodicy: the root of evil is not humanity, but a rebel angel.

Semjaza & the Oath

The leader Semjaza (also Shemihaza) is uniquely portrayed as morally complex. Before descending, he expresses doubt to his fellow Watchers: "I fear you will not agree to do this deed, and I alone will have to pay the penalty." The others swear a binding oath together on the summit of Mount Hermon — the oath of collective rebellion — absolving any single angel of sole blame. Unlike Azazel, who is bound in the earth, Semjaza is bound in the stars (Ch. 18), forced to watch his fallen world from above until judgment.

Enoch as Cosmic Scribe

Throughout 1 Enoch, the patriarch himself is not merely a passive witness. He is designated "the scribe of righteousness" (1 Enoch 12:4) — a celestial notary who records the deeds of all, good and evil, to be presented at the final judgment. This identity as divine record-keeper becomes the seed of the later Jewish concept of Metatron (3 Enoch) and forms a direct parallel to the role ascribed to Jesus as the divine Logos in John 1 — the pre-existent being through whom all of God's purposes are mediated and articulated.

7. Modern Scholarly Consensus & Legacy

Today, the scholarly consensus (led by figures like Milik, VanderKam, and Nickelsburg) views 1 Enoch not as a fringe product, but as:

A Primary Mirror of Second Temple Judaism

Proving ancient Judaism was a diverse, highly creative theological sandbox filled with competing calendars, complex angelologies, and deep cosmic mythologies.

The Direct Cradle of Christian Christology

Without 1 Enoch (specifically the Parables), the transition from the Old Testament’s nationalistic messianism to the New Testament's cosmic, pre-existent, judging Savior is incredibly difficult to map.

The Key to Ancient Demonology

The primary text explaining why the New Testament assumes an active spirit world filled with demons and spiritual warfare—concepts absent in the Hebrew Bible but fully systematized in 1 Enoch.
