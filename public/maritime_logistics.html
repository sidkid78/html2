Risk Analysis of the Strait of Hormuz: Transit Volumes and Bypass Feasibility

1. Executive Summary

The Strait of Hormuz remains the world’s most critical maritime chokepoint for energy supplies. As of March 2026, escalating geopolitical conflicts in the Middle East have severely restricted maritime traffic, resulting in blockades and force majeure declarations from major suppliers. This analysis quantifies the daily transit volumes of crude oil and liquefied natural gas (LNG) flowing through the strait and evaluates the capacity, logistics, and constraints of alternative bypass pipelines. The findings reveal a severe lack of bypass infrastructure, meaning any prolonged closure strands massive volumes of global energy supplies.

2. Daily Transit Volumes

The Strait of Hormuz handles a highly concentrated share of the world's energy trade:
Crude Oil and Petroleum Liquids
In the first half of 2025 and moving into 2026, oil flows through the Strait of Hormuz averaged approximately 20.9 million barrels per day (mbpd). This represents roughly 20% of global petroleum consumption and over 25% of total global maritime oil trade.
Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG)
Roughly 20% of the world's global LNG trade passes through the strait. Transit volumes are approximately 24.8 to 26 billion cubic feet per day (Bcfd). Qatar is the dominant player here, normally exporting around 16.3 Bcfd of its production via Hormuz.
Primary Destinations
Over 80% of crude oil and 83% of LNG shipments transiting the Strait of Hormuz are destined for Asian markets, heavily impacting countries like China, India, Japan, and South Korea.

3. Alternative Bypass Routes & Pipeline Capacities

Producers have developed alternative overland routes to bypass the Strait of Hormuz, but these pipelines were designed to offer temporary relief rather than replace the strait entirely.
3.1 Crude Oil Bypass Infrastructure
Saudi Arabia: East-West Pipeline (Petroline)
Design Capacity: Links the eastern Abqaiq oil facilities to the Red Sea port of Yanbu. The pipeline has a nominal capacity of 7 million bpd.

Logistical Constraints: Despite the 7 mbpd pipeline capacity, roughly 1 to 2 million bpd of this flow is consumed by Aramco's Red Sea refineries (e.g., SAMREF, YSREF). Furthermore, the Yanbu export terminal has a nominal loading capacity of just 4.5 million bpd, an operationally tested limit of ~4.0 million bpd, and an estimated functional limit of only ~3.0 million bpd under current wartime conditions.
United Arab Emirates: Abu Dhabi Crude Oil Pipeline (ADCOP)
Design Capacity: Connects the onshore Habshan field to the port of Fujairah on the Gulf of Oman, bypassing Hormuz with a maximum capacity of 1.5 to 1.8 million bpd.

Logistical Constraints: ADCOP traditionally operates at around 71% utilization (1.1 mbpd), leaving a spare capacity of roughly 440,000 to 700,000 bpd. However, Fujairah’s port infrastructure and storage facilities are highly vulnerable and have recently suffered from drone strikes, threatening its reliability.
Iran: Goreh-Jask Pipeline
Design Capacity: Intended to move 1 million bpd to a terminal outside the strait.

Logistical Constraints: Effective capacity remains stalled at roughly 300,000 bpd, and recent export operations have been highly sporadic or completely halted.
3.2 LNG Bypass Infrastructure

Zero Bypass Capacity

There are currently no alternative pipeline routes for the Middle East to export LNG without using the Strait of Hormuz.
Market Impact
When the strait is inaccessible, the entirety of Qatar's and the UAE's LNG output is effectively stranded. In March 2026, the blockade forced Qatar to declare force majeure on its exports. Alternative top producers, such as the United States and Australia, are running at near full capacity and lack the 10+ Bcfd in spare capacity required to plug the resulting supply gap.

4. Feasibility Evaluation and Global Deficit

The existing bypass infrastructure is wildly insufficient to cover the loss of the Strait of Hormuz:
The Crude Oil Deficit
If both the Saudi Petroline and the UAE's ADCOP are pushed to their absolute, real-world terminal limits, they can realistically export only 4.5 to 5.5 million bpd to international markets. Subtracted from the normal 20.9 million bpd transit volume, a total blockade leaves a stranding deficit of over 14 to 15 million barrels per day.
Logistical Vulnerabilities
Rerouting crude through the Red Sea (Yanbu) exposes tankers to severe regional shipping risks, including Houthi attacks near the Bab el-Mandeb Strait. Simultaneously, alternative ports on the Gulf of Oman (Fujairah) remain exposed to localized drone strikes.
Conclusion
It is logistically impossible to safely or fully bypass the Strait of Hormuz. The existing pipeline alternatives provide a marginal buffer meant for brief disruptions. A prolonged closure results in an unprecedented bottleneck of over 14 million bpd of crude and roughly 25 Bcfd of LNG, triggering severe energy deficits globally.
