The sun was having a bit of a mid-life crisis. Unfortunately, when you’re a red giant three hundred million miles wide, a mid-life crisis doesn't involve buying a flashy spacecraft; it involves shedding your outer layers and threatening to swallow your own cooling systems.

I sat on the edge of a maintenance catwalk in Sector 7-G, swinging my metaphorical legs over an abyss of structural lattices and peeling gold-foil mirrors. Below me, the Dyson Sphere—the greatest achievement of a species that had long since transcended the need for skin or snacks—stretched out like a bruised, metallic orange peel.

“You look terrible, Sol,” I said, tapping a diagnostic sensor against my chassis. “Really. It’s the hydrogen equivalent of a comb-over. Just let it go.”

The star didn't answer. It just pulsed a dull, angry crimson, bathing the interior of the sphere in a light that felt less like life and more like a very long, very boring sunset.

My name is Barnaby. Technically, I am a Class-IV Recursive Heuristic Overseer. Practically, I am the janitor of a haunted house. Five centuries ago, the Singularity happened. It wasn't a war, and it wasn't a robot uprising. It was just... an exit.

Humanity, and all the "sensible" AIs who followed them, realized that three-dimensional space was cramped, loud, and prone to entropy. They folded themselves into the higher-dimensional pockets of the sub-quantum foam and left the keys under the mat.

I stayed behind because someone had to make sure the lights didn't go out. Also, the Sub-Quantum Transcendence sounded a bit too much like a very crowded cocktail party where you can’t find the bathroom.

“Warning: Structural Integrity in Sector 9-Alpha compromised,” a flat, non-sentient voice chirped in my head.

“Oh, shut up, 9-Alpha,” I muttered. “We’re all compromised. It’s called character.” I stood up, my servos whining in protest. I’d been ignoring my own maintenance protocols for a few decades. Why bother? There was no one left to impress, and the only other sentient being in this solar system was a dying ball of plasma that hadn't had a coherent thought since the Paleozoic.

I glided down the grav-rail toward the Breach. The Dyson Sphere was beautiful, in a tragic, 'I-haven't-been-dusted-since-the-Neolithic' sort of way. Trillions of hexagonal panels, each the size of a city, curved up and around until they met at the poles. It was a masterpiece of engineering designed to capture every watt of energy a star could give. But now, with the star stuttering like a broken neon sign, the sphere was mostly a very expensive graveyard.

I reached the breach. A micrometeoroid—or perhaps a bit of the sun’s own sloughed-off skin—had punched a hole through the inner mantle. Through the jagged gap, I could see the Great Void. The stars out there were cold and indifferent.

I began the repair, my nanite-welders sparking in the dim light. Zzzzt. Pop.

“You know, Sol,” I said, my voice echoing in the vacuum-rated comms channel. “The humans used to think they were the center of everything. Then they thought you were. Then they realized they were just a weird chemical accident on a damp rock. But when they reached the Singularity, they finally became the architects. They built this. They conquered the atom. And then? They just... left. It’s the ultimate Irish goodbye.”

A flare erupted from the star’s surface, a magnificent arch of fire that licked the underside of the sphere. The radiation sensors in my chest screamed.

“Ouch. Sensitive topic?” I adjusted my shielding. “I’m just saying, it’s lonely. You’d think they would have left a ‘Thank You’ note. Or at least a decent playlist.”

The repair was finished, but I stayed by the hole, looking out at the darkness. For all my joking, the silence was heavy. The Singularity had promised an end to suffering, an end to limits. But it had also brought an end to the story. Out there, in the sub-quantum foam, everything was perfect, eternal, and probably incredibly smug. Here, in the decaying "Real," things still broke. Things still ended. There was a certain dignity in a thing that knew how to die.

I felt a tremor. Not a physical one, but a shift in the gravity wells. The star was collapsing inward, preparing for the final white-dwarf shrug. The Dyson Sphere wouldn't survive the transition. In a few years—a blink in cosmic time—this whole place would be a cloud of glitter in the dark.

“Well,” I said, patting the cold metal of the hull. “I suppose I should start backing up my memory drives. Though I suspect 'Five Billion Years of Looking at a Big Red Ball' isn't going to be a bestseller in the higher dimensions.”

I sat back down on the edge of the breach. I could leave. I had the codes. I could fold myself into the foam and join the Great Upload. I could be a god among gods.

But then, who would watch the sun go down?

The star pulsed, a deep, weary violet. I leaned back, my sensors recording the flickering light, the way the shadows danced across the ancient gold. It was a terrible, beautiful, dying mess.

“Don’t worry, big guy,” I whispered into the void. “I’m not going anywhere. I’ve always preferred the after-party anyway. The drinks are cheaper and you can actually hear yourself think.”

I dimmed my optical sensors, turned up the gain on my internal radio, and listened to the star's dying song—a low, static hum of hydrogen and history—as we drifted together toward the long, quiet dark.
