Title:

The Artist’s Guide to the Fight: Killing the "Big Lie" of Live Music

1. The Big Lie and The Bleed-Out

You know the feeling. It’s 2:00 AM. You’ve just played your heart out. You loaded the van, you packed the room, your fans drank the bar dry. But when you walk into the back office to get paid, the promoter hands you $40 and a bullshit excuse.

Welcome to the "Big Lie" of live music: the delusion that just because you booked a gig, you’re going to get a fair deal.

For decades, the live music industry has operated on a predatory model called the Risk Shift. Venues and promoters protect themselves by pushing all the financial danger—hidden sound fees, phantom marketing costs, room rentals—onto your shoulders. You aren’t treated as a partner. You are treated as an unpaid laborer funding their overhead.

You’ve been operating on "blind hope" because the industry kept you in the dark. It’s time to turn the lights on.

2. The Settlement Waterfall

(Or: How Your Money is Stolen)

When a fan buys a $15 ticket to see you, that money doesn't go to you. It gets dropped into what the industry calls the "Settlement Waterfall." We call it the Bleed-Out.

Before you see a single dime, the platform takes a cut. Then the house takes a cut for the sound guy. Then the door guy. Then the lights. Suddenly, a packed room yields exactly zero profit.

They inflate their costs because they know you can’t verify them. They hide behind vague "Production Fees" so they never have to show you the math. But the moment you demand to see where every single dollar goes, their scam collapses.

Weaponizing the Math

3. Your Ultimate Bullshit Detector

You didn't get into music to be an accountant. You got into it to make art. But in this industry, if you don't know your math, your art will be bled dry.

Before you ever load a single amp, you need to know your Break-Even Point. It’s the exact number of tickets required to cover the house costs. If you bring 50 fans, but the venue’s hidden fees require 80 tickets just to break even, you are paying them to play.

Math is your ultimate bullshit detector. When you use a transparent partner—like Jonathan Kruse, who shares daily operating costs upfront—the risk is shared. When a venue refuses to show you an itemized bill, they are planning to rob you. It’s that simple. Walk away.

4. Evolution

From Begging To Commanding

Stop asking for permission to play. You are the one generating the revenue.

When you use the FairGig data to model your shows, you transform from a desperate artist into a dangerous negotiator. You don't guess; you know. You know exactly what happens to your payout if the room is empty, if it's typical, or if it sells out.

If you know you bring 200 fans to a room, you aren't just "booking a date." You are bringing a stack of cash to their bar. You use that data as a weapon to demand a higher split or a guaranteed payout. You stop being the venue's victim and become the CEO of your own stage.

5. The Ecosystem

THE TRUTH TAX

Starving the Predators

For too long, bad venues have survived because bands didn't talk to each other. We are changing that. We are building the Reputation Layer.

We are enforcing a "Truth Tax" on the industry. If a venue hides their fees, rips off bands, or changes the deal at the end of the night, it becomes a permanent, flashing red flag in the FairGig ecosystem. We will starve opaque venues of the one thing they need to survive: top-tier talent.

6. Conclusion

Protect Your Sanctuary

Cliff Franco spent 15 years fronting bands like The Pursuit and The Struggs. He learned the hardest lesson of the industry: Music is a sacred, personal sanctuary. It is your craft, your soul, and your voice.

But if you don't defend the business side with cold, hard data, the industry will crush your sanctuary.

FairGig exists for one reason: to protect the art. When you master the math of show economics, you spend zero time worrying about scams, and 100% of your time destroying the stage.

No more hidden fees.
